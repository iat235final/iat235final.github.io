CASE STUDIES

While it is important to display quantitative data about the success of Portugal's drug policy, it is equally important to show the qualitative aspect of it. This page takes a look at 3 individuals whose live has been affected by the policy; Bruno, Pedro and Elvira.

Bruno

Bruno is a young Portugese man who was caught with some hashish, a drug made from the resin of the cannabis plant. Upon being found with possession, he had to make an apperance before the Commission for the Dissuasion of Drug Addiction; a state mandated early intervention.  

"This isn't a court, and I'm not a judge.." Says one of the members. He adds that he doesn't think about the fines he's going to apply, how many people he's going to threaten. Without these steps, he says that it is easy to focus on health issues and what else they can provide.

The punishment Bruno received for smoking 4 joints of hashish a day was simply a warning, along with an offer of information about treatment programs. The members found that one out of ten people who visit the commission have a serious drug problem and for them this visit can be the first step in getting treatment. "We found that in Portugal, after 15 years, punishing users for using does not work. If you increase the penalty, does that usage decrease? It doesn't." The member adds.

PEDRO

According to the documentary, the cornerstone of Portugal's treatment and rehab model is now outreach and engagement. Pedro is a homeless heroin addict living in East Lisbon where teams normally make their daily rounds. Two psychology graduates visit Pedro and ask him:  

"One piece or two?" In regards to how many needles and safe heroin Pedro needed that day to not fall sick through contamination or impurities. "Do you want more than two? Do you want the wipes?" The outreach team adds.

These outreach team members meet the same people struggling with drug addiction. They know their stories and they providea very human connection to the health care system.

"I know theres a lot of drugs on this alley in the city." The Pedro states, referring to the drug epidemic in Canada, particularly Vancouver. He appreciates that people are keeping him alive. "Their job is very important, they are very brave, they come here, help people pick up the needles, no security, they're alone. They are very brave and its very important." He adds.

ELVIRA

Elvira is a 50 year old Portugese lady who is a recovered addict. She works as a cleaning lady and states that she is pleased with the mobile methadone program.

"Sometimes I am near this van, sometimes I am near another van, I go there so it's easy to get methadone." She states. Methadone is a commonly prescribed substitute for heroin that is administered by recovering drug addicts. It eases the withdrawal symptoms without causing the euphoric high.

"I live my life normally, I feel okay, I clean my house, I take care of my grandchildren, I work, I am okay. I don't need to go buy the drugs anymore. She adds, alluding to the success of Portugal's drug policy that enable individuals like Elvira to find hope in the country as a drug addict by access such programs that are very publicly available.

Methadoen treatment is practiced widely including in other countries like Cananda, but what is striking about the Portugese system is that it puts in effort in getting it to the people and taking the stigma out of using it.
