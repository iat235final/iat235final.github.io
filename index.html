Drug

Decriminalisation

in Portugal

 
In 2001, Portugal was experiencing an opioid-involved overdose crisis. The country used criminalization and incarceration to try to manage drug use, while HIV rates among people who use drugs were the highest in Europe. In response to this emergency, Portugal launched its decriminalization program that year, and the rest is history. Overdose deaths have plummeted by 80 percent, while the percentage of drug users diagnosed with new HIV infections fell from 52 percent in 2000 to 7 percent in 2015. Rates of problematic drug use and drug-related incarceration have also fallen, while numbers of people voluntarily entering treatment for substance use issues have increased.

POLICY EFFECTIVENESS

Select a chart to view

According to EMCDDA (European Monitoring Centre for Drugs and Drug Addiction), it can be shown that drug use and prevalance has decreased from the time the policy was put into place in 2001. This trend can be marked in this analysis through 3 datasets: number of drug related overdoses, the increase of drug-related infectious diseases (HIV and AIDS). Data on drug-related infectious diseases are available from drug treatment facilities and provide insights into some subgroups of drug users: (i) those demanding treatment for the first time at the public network of outpatient treatment facilities; (ii) those admitted to public detoxification treatment units or certified private detoxification units; and (iii) those in treatment in public or certified private therapeutic communities.
